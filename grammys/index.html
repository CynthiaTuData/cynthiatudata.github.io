The Grammy Awards’ Diversity Problem, in Numbers

Have artists of color received due recognition at the music industry’s most prestigious awards?

By Cynthia Tu

Feb 18, 2023

The Grammys got canceled, again.

The 2023 Grammy awards ceremony closed out with fan’s outrage for its lack of diversity, which reached the climax when Harry Styles took home the night’s most prestigious award “Album of the Year.” Despite the shock that Styles beat Beyonce, who was most-anticipated to win the award, Harry Styles’ victory speech came as an even bigger shock to fans.

“This doesn’t happen to people like me very often and this is so, so nice. Thank you very much.”

The pop star immediately received backlash online. Sam Sanders, a former NPR podcast host tweeted that Styles' speech "is the most white privilege-iest thing to ever be uttered at an awards show ever for all time.”

Harry Styles' controversial speech certainly wasn't the only reason why the Grammys and the Recording Academy came under fire. The institution has been criticized for its lack of diversity for quite some time.

Many artists of color had publicly declared their disdain for the award’s lack of inclusion. Musicians such as 50 Cent, Kanye West and Jay-Z had “boycotted” the award by not attending the ceremony despite being nominated (and winning in some cases). In 2017, singer Frank Ocean even called out that the Grammys "certainly has nostalgic importance.” during an interview with the New York Times.

“It just doesn’t seem to be representing very well for people who come from where I come from, and hold down what I hold down. I think the infrastructure of the awarding system and the nomination system and screening system is dated.” Ocean said.

From 1990 to present, a little more than 40% of all general field nominees have been artists of color. ( “Song of the Year” category is omitted because it is awarded to the songwriter, not the artists).

White nominees also stand a higher chance of winning than non-white nominees.

Not only did artists of color receive fewer nominations in the main categories, they were also less likely to win an award after being nominated. From 1990 to 2023, the Grammys selected 102 winners among 568 nominations in these categories. While 21% of white nominees successfully brought home an trophy, only 14% of non-white nominees ended up winning.

So how does the Grammys nomination pipeline work?

In order to be nominated for the award, a musician's work must first be submitted for consideration. Every year, members of the Recording Academy can recommend releases for the Grammys.

In 2020, the Academy disclosed its "diversity and inclusion numbers" during an industry summit #ChangeMusic. According to the report, of all 14,553 academy members, 26% were female and 69% were male (6% were reported as unknown).

As to racial diversity, the academy reported 11% of its voting members were Black. The Academy did not disclose data for other members invloved in the voting process.

Only in recent years had the Grammys attempted to make moves to improve its inclusion issue.

Prior to the 2022 award season, a committee of 15-30 members had the final say on the selection of nominees and winners. This mechanism was essentially a black box and allowed for corruption and bias. An 2020 report by the Rolling Stone revealed that members of the final committee would "shoehorn in" artists who weren't selected by the general voting memership, and even engaged in "financial lobbying for votes," according to sources inside of the Recording Academy.

The academy changed its rules last year so that entries in the general categories will go through a peer-reviewed, majority vote process.

The recording academy also launched a “Inclusion Rider” Program last year to “recognize the value of people from under-represented backgrounds and create space for increased and meaningful representation for everyone in the music community,” according to the Grammys' website.

“Diversity, equity and representation inclusion that's really at the heart of everything we're trying to do at the Academy,” Harvey Mason Jr, CEO of the Recording Academy, told Yahoo Finance in an interview. “So it will be manifested on our stage in front of the camera, behind the camera.”

The “Inclusion Rider” program states that the recording academy will “strive” to ensure that one-third of its on-stage and off-stage employees will be from “under-represented communities,” which they defined as women and people of color—essentially, anyone except for white men.
